Free Online Fade In & Fade Out Tool

An audio clip that starts or ends abruptly can sound jarring — a sudden burst of sound at the start, or a hard cutoff at the end that feels unfinished. Adding a fade in and fade out smooths both of these moments out, gradually raising the volume from silence at the beginning and lowering it back to silence at the end, for a cleaner, more polished result.

This tool applies fades directly in your browser. Your audio file is decoded, the fade curves are applied to the start and end, and the result is handed back to you as a download — nothing is uploaded to a server.

How Fades Work

A fade gradually adjusts volume over a chosen duration rather than changing it instantly. A fade in starts at silence and smoothly ramps up to full volume over however many seconds you set; a fade out does the reverse at the end of the clip, smoothly ramping down from full volume to silence. Because the transition is gradual rather than instant, there's no audible "click" or jump the way there can be with an unfaded, hard cut.

When to Use Fades

  • Trimmed clips: if you've cut a section out of a longer recording, a short fade at the start and end hides any abruptness from the edit point
  • Podcast intros and outros: a fade-in on intro music, and a fade-out as it transitions to speech, sounds far more professional than an instant cut
  • Background music: music under a voiceover often fades in at the start and fades out at the end so it doesn't compete with the speech
  • Looping audio: a subtle fade at both ends can make a looped clip feel less like it's abruptly restarting

Choosing Fade Length

Short fades (0.5-1.5 seconds) work well for tightening up a trimmed clip or smoothing a quick transition without the listener really noticing the fade itself happening. Longer fades (3-6 seconds or more) are more common for music tracks or ambient background audio, where a slow, gradual fade feels more natural and intentional. As a general guide, shorter clips usually call for shorter fades, while longer, more atmospheric material can support longer ones.

Tips for Best Results

  • Preview your fades before downloading — what sounds right varies a lot depending on the specific clip and its content
  • If a clip already starts or ends on a very loud sound, a slightly longer fade can help avoid an abrupt loudness drop-off
  • Setting either fade duration to 0 disables that fade entirely, in case you only need one side adjusted
What is a fade in and fade out? ▾
A fade in gradually raises the volume from silence at the start of a clip, and a fade out gradually lowers it to silence at the end, instead of the audio starting or stopping abruptly.
Why do audio clips need fades? ▾
Without a fade, a clip that starts or ends mid-sound can produce an audible click or an abrupt cutoff. Fades smooth this out for a more polished, professional-sounding result.
Can I set different fade lengths for the start and end? ▾
Yes, the fade-in and fade-out durations are controlled independently, so you can set them to different lengths or disable either one.
Is my audio file uploaded to a server? ▾
No. All fading is applied locally in your browser. Your file is never uploaded anywhere.
