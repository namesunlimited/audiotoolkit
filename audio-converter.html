Free Online Audio Converter

Every audio format exists for a reason. MP3 keeps files small enough to share and stream easily. WAV keeps every bit of the original recording intact, uncompressed. M4A balances quality and size for Apple devices. But when the format you have doesn't match the format you need — a video editor that only accepts WAV, a phone that won't play M4A, a podcast host that requires MP3 — you need a fast way to switch between them without losing quality or installing new software.

This tool converts audio files directly in your browser. There's no upload to a server, no waiting in a queue, and no account required. You pick a file, choose a format, and download the result — usually within seconds.

How This Audio Converter Works

Unlike most "online converters," which quietly upload your file to a server somewhere and process it remotely, this tool does everything using your browser's own built-in audio engine (the Web Audio API). Your file is read directly from your device, decoded into raw audio data, re-encoded into your chosen format, and handed back to you as a download — all without a single byte leaving your computer or phone.

That matters for two reasons. First, privacy: recordings can be personal — voice memos, interviews, unreleased music — and keeping them off third-party servers avoids any question of who else might see or store them. Second, speed: because there's no upload and no server queue, conversion is typically limited only by your own device's processing power, which for most audio files takes just a few seconds.

MP3 vs. WAV: Which Should You Choose?

If you're not sure which format to convert to, here's the practical difference:

Choose MP3 when:

  • You want a smaller file size for sharing, emailing, or uploading elsewhere
  • You're creating a podcast episode, voice note, or music file for everyday listening
  • The destination app or device doesn't support WAV

Choose WAV when:

  • You need the highest possible quality, with zero compression artifacts
  • You're importing the audio into editing software for further work
  • File size doesn't matter as much as preserving every detail of the original recording

A good rule of thumb: convert to WAV if the audio still has more editing ahead of it, and convert to MP3 once it's in its final form and ready to share.

Choosing an MP3 Bitrate

When converting to MP3, you'll also pick a bitrate — essentially how much data is used per second of audio, which directly affects both file size and quality:

  • 128 kbps — noticeably smaller files, fine for spoken word or casual listening
  • 192 kbps — a solid middle ground for most music and podcasts
  • 256 kbps — closer to "transparent" quality, hard to distinguish from the original for most listeners
  • 320 kbps — the maximum standard MP3 bitrate, the closest MP3 gets to lossless

If you're archiving music or working with something you'll want to keep long-term, 256 or 320 kbps is a safe choice. For voice recordings or quick shares, 128 or 192 kbps will save space without much noticeable difference.

Common Uses for Audio Conversion

People convert audio files for all kinds of everyday reasons: turning a WAV recording from a voice recorder into MP3 for easier sharing, converting a lossless music file to a smaller format before uploading it somewhere with storage limits, or preparing a file to be compatible with an older device or specific piece of software.

Podcasters often need to convert raw WAV recordings into MP3 before publishing, since most podcast hosting platforms expect compressed audio. Musicians sometimes need the reverse — converting a compressed MP3 demo into WAV so it can be layered into a Digital Audio Workstation (DAW) without stacking further compression on top of it.

Tips for Best Results

  • Start from the highest-quality source file you have — converting a low-quality MP3 to WAV won't add back detail that's already been lost
  • If you plan to edit the audio further, convert to WAV first and do your final MP3 export at the end
  • For voice recordings meant for transcription or note-taking, a lower MP3 bitrate is usually perfectly fine and saves space
  • Keep a copy of your original file until you've confirmed the converted version sounds right
